## Part A — Content Critique

### Overview
**Strengths:**
- "What It Is" is clear and non-technical. A practice owner could read it and explain Social Stream to a colleague.
- "The Problem It Solves" uses specific, relatable imagery ("Facebook page hasn't been touched since 2023," "Instagram was set up but never posted to").
- "How It Works" is 4 concrete steps, not abstract.
- Important Distinctions clearly separates Social Stream from Campaigns, DIY tools, and generic content.

**Weaknesses found:**
1. "How It Works" step 2 says "Streams subscribe automatically" — but the sentence that follows says "Practices offering specialty services can subscribe to specialty streams." This creates ambiguity: do practices auto-subscribe or manually subscribe? Needs clarification from Lenny.
2. Missing: no mention of the CTA link configuration (Facebook and Google posts can include a link to the practice website). This is a feature worth noting in the overview.

### Benefits
**Strengths:**
- Each benefit is specific and addressable — not vague ("Social Media Without a Social Media Manager" is concrete).
- Pain points are persona-aligned (Dr. Someday → abandoned Facebook; Sarah → Canva frustration; Growth Chaser → multi-location consistency).
- The Dry Eye specialty stream benefit (#4) is well-supported by data (18.8 interactions per location per post).
- ROI summary includes "potential Instagram reach if fully connected" — forward-looking value.

**Weaknesses found:**
1. Benefit #6 (Google Business Profile for Local SEO) makes an SEO claim ("regular GBP posts signal activity to Google's local search algorithm") that is [INFERRED], not [OBSERVED]. This needs qualifying language — "may support local SEO signals" rather than a definitive claim.
2. The "400 Google clicks" stat is notably small compared to Facebook (174,401) and Instagram (39,708). Using it as a standalone benefit might invite skepticism. Consider framing Google clicks alongside the broader GBP visibility benefit rather than leading with the number.

### Competitive
**Strengths:**
- Honest about the blank canvas problem — doesn't pretend Hootsuite is bad, just identifies why practices don't use it.
- The "content vs. tool" framing is the clearest competitive distinction. This should be the lead competitive message.
- Objection handling is practical and addresses real concerns (especially #4: "automated posts feel inauthentic" and #7: "social media doesn't drive appointments").

**Weaknesses found:**
1. The competitive file doesn't address **Meta's algorithmic suppression of business page organic reach**. Facebook organic reach for business pages has been declining for years. This affects the value proposition of automated Facebook posting — if organic reach is low, are 174,401 impressions actually reaching patients? This should at least be acknowledged.
2. Missing: no mention of TikTok. While TikTok isn't part of Social Stream, a practice owner might ask "but TikTok is where the attention is now." The competitive file should preemptively address why Social Stream focuses on Facebook, Instagram, and GBP rather than TikTok.

### Videoscript
**Strengths:**
- Opening is strong and relatable ("When was the last time your practice posted to social media?"). Direct, no preamble.
- Visual progression works: abandoned pages → Social Stream calendar → network scale → specialty streams → CTA.
- Length is appropriate (85 seconds total).

**Weaknesses found:**
1. The "Scale" section (0:50–1:05) jumps from practice-level view to admin network reports. This is an internal-facing view that a practice prospect wouldn't see. Consider reframing as "across nearly 2,700 practices, our content team published..." rather than showing the admin dashboard.
2. No mention of platform-optimized images (separate FB/IG formats) in the script. This is a quality differentiator worth a visual beat.

### Screenshotbrief
**Strengths:**
- 7 shots covering practice view, admin reports, connections, post detail, and connection warning.
- Each shot has clear setup instructions and "why it matters" context.
- Shot 1 (practice calendar) is correctly identified as the hero.
- Shot 4 (connections) tells the growth story — smart inclusion.

**Weaknesses found:**
1. Missing: no screenshot of a Social Stream post as it appears on Facebook. A screenshot showing the post in-context (on a practice's actual Facebook page) would be powerful proof that the content looks professional and native. This is the "resulting view" that patients see.
2. Shot 7 (admin calendar) is marked optional — but it shows the admin creating content that reaches 731 practices. This is the "multiplier" visual and should be required.

### Casestudy
**Strengths:**
- Smart decision to make this a network case study rather than forcing a per-practice story that doesn't exist for Social Posts.
- Stream spotlight (EyeCarePro main) provides a concrete "4 posts → 53,131 interactions → 731 locations" story.
- Dry Eye specialty stream spotlight proves specialty content performs equally well.
- Connection opportunity section correctly identifies the growth angle.

**Weaknesses found:**
1. The composite quote at the bottom feels generic. "Our page has never been this active" could be about any social tool. Consider making it more specific to Social Stream's automated nature: reference the calendar filling itself or content appearing without the practice knowing it was scheduled.
2. Revenue math section is weak for Social Posts. The "cost avoidance" framing ($1,446,750/month if all practices paid an agency) is theoretically valid but feels forced. Social media posting cost avoidance is less compelling than Campaigns' direct appointment attribution. Consider trimming this section.

### Pagebrief
**Strengths:**
- Headline ("Your social media hasn't been updated in months. Let's fix that — automatically.") is specific and problem-first. Strong.
- Alt headline is good for A/B testing.
- SEO section is complete (title 66 chars, meta desc 155 chars, primary + secondary keywords).
- Problem cards are specific and emotional (especially "The Abandoned Facebook Page").
- How It Works is genuinely 3 steps — simpler than Campaigns' workflow.

**Weaknesses found:**
1. The page structure has 4 alternating feature pairs. The Campaigns page also used alternating pairs. Consider whether Social Posts needs all 4, or if 3 would be tighter (since Social Posts is a simpler feature than Campaigns).
2. The comparison table uses "Doing Nothing" as a column. While honest, it's an unusual competitor to include. Consider replacing with "Doctor Multimedia" or "Generic Social Posting" to address the "bad automated social" competitor directly.
3. Meta description is 155 characters — right at the limit. Safe but might get truncated on some search engines. Consider tightening to 145-150.

## Part C — Process Critique

### Process Improvement #1
**File:** step3_case_study_deep_dive.md
**Issue:** Social Posts doesn't have the same per-practice case study structure as Campaigns. The Step 3 methodology was designed for features with measurable per-practice outcomes (email → appointment). For features like Social Posts where the value is network-level and ambient, the step3 format needed significant adaptation.
**Suggested change:** Add a note to the research methodology: "For features where the value is network-level or ambient (no direct per-practice ROI), adapt Step 3 to focus on network case studies, stream-level spotlights, and connection opportunity analysis rather than forcing a per-practice deep dive."

### Process Improvement #2
**File:** step4_resulting_view.md
**Issue:** The "resulting view" for Social Posts is the patient's social media feed — which we can't observe from the admin dashboard. Unlike Campaigns (where we can preview the landing page, email, and social posts), the actual patient-facing result of Social Posts requires accessing a practice's real Facebook/Instagram page.
**Suggested change:** Add to Step 4 instructions: "For social-only features, the resulting view may require observing the output on the actual social platform (e.g., a practice's Facebook page) rather than within the PatientEngage admin. Plan browser time to visit 1-2 practice Facebook pages to capture the final posted content."

### Process Improvement #3
**File:** step6_pregen_questions.md
**Issue:** Social Posts requires different questions than Campaigns. The "killer stat" question worked for Campaigns because there were dramatic per-practice numbers (80 appointments). For Social Posts, the stat candidates are all network-level and less dramatic individually. The question framework should adapt to the feature's value structure.
**Suggested change:** Modify Q2 to include: "If the feature doesn't have dramatic per-practice metrics, what alternative framing should the killer stat use? (e.g., network scale, multiplier effect, cost avoidance, or connection opportunity)"
